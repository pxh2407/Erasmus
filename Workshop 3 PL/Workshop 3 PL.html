Poland · Workshop 3

Should the EU Create a Common Army?

A decision-making game workshop on European security — seniors explore EU defence cooperation, analyse consequences and develop civic dialogue skills.

Author: Maja Huminiłowicz · Fundacja „Aktywni XXI"

Duration 135 min Participants 10–25 people Audience Seniors 60+

About the Workshop

"The aim of this workshop is not to persuade participants to adopt a single opinion, but to jointly reflect on the possible consequences of different decisions concerning Europe's future."

Workshop Objectives

General Objectives

  • 1Increase knowledge of contemporary security challenges in Europe.
  • 2Develop civic competencies through analysis of possible directions for defence cooperation in the EU.
  • 3Support a culture of dialogue and constructive discussion on public issues.

Specific Objectives

  • 1Understand the basic principles of defence cooperation in the European Union.
  • 2Identify potential benefits and risks associated with creating a common EU army.
  • 3Analyse the impact of security decisions on citizens' daily lives.
  • 4Develop argumentation skills and the ability to recognise different perspectives.
  • 5Strengthen the sense of agency and competence in public debate.

Who Is This Workshop For?

People aged 60+ with diverse educational and professional backgrounds, interested in social, civic and European issues. No specialist knowledge of politics, security or the EU is required.

Working Methods

  • 1Interactive mini-lecture
  • 2Moderated discussion
  • 3Brainstorming
  • 4Group work
  • 5Decision-making game (without role-playing)
  • 6Individual and group reflection

How the Workshop Unfolds (approx. 135 min)

  1. 1 10 min

    Introduction

    Welcome participants, explain the purpose and ground rules. Opening question: "What gives you a sense of security in Europe today?" Collect responses on the board.

  2. 2 20 min

    Activity 1 — Security: What Does It Mean Today?

    Moderated discussion on the broad meaning of security. Questions: What does "security" mean today? Is security only about the military? Are countries safer together or separately?

  3. 3 15 min

    Activity 2 — Introduction to the Idea of a Common EU Army

    Short interactive mini-lecture on EU defence cooperation: what it is, what a common army could look like, and the four main dilemmas — Security, Costs, Sovereignty, Decisions.

  4. 4 40 min

    Activity 3 — Decision-Making Game "Europe Under Pressure"

    Participants work in groups of 3–5 and make decisions based on 5 game rounds (see below). After each round, groups present their choices. At the end, each group answers YES / NO / YES, BUT… and completes the sentence.

  5. 5 20 min

    Activity 4 — Reflective Discussion

    What was most difficult? What was most important — security, costs or independence? Optional final vote: more in favour / more against / undecided.

  6. 6 10 min

    Summary and Closing

    Key conclusions: there is no single simple answer; different interests must be balanced; cooperation and trust are important. Final exercise: "For Europe's security, the most important thing is…"

The Decision-Making Game — "Europe Under Pressure"

5 rounds. In each round, groups discuss a scenario and choose one of the options. After all rounds, each group completes the final sentence.

R1

Round 1 — Europe Feels Less Secure

Tensions are rising along the borders of the European Union. Many people are beginning to wonder whether Europe can ensure its own security.

A. Countries act separately
B. Greater cooperation between countries
C. A common EU army

R2

Round 2 — The Cost of Security

Experts report that creating a common EU army would be very costly and would take many years.

A. Security is the top priority — it is worth investing in
B. Yes, but only if pensions, healthcare and social services are not affected
C. Each country should decide on its own spending

R3

Round 3 — Who Decides?

Countries cannot agree on who should command the common army.

A. Each country retains control over its military
B. Joint decisions, but with national oversight
C. One common EU command

R4

Round 4 — Citizens' Concerns

Some citizens fear losing independence and taking on new obligations.

A. A common army for defence only
B. Only with the consent of all member states
C. Gradual cooperation instead of a single army

R5

Round 5 — Final Decision

The European Union must make a decision.

YES  /  NO  /  YES, BUT…

Complete the sentence: "A common EU army would make sense only if…"
