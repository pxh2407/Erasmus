Poland · Workshop 2

Should Poland Adopt the Euro?

An Oxford-style debate workshop on the pros and cons of adopting the euro — seniors develop argumentation, critical thinking and civic dialogue skills.

Author: Danuta Sadownik · Fundacja „Aktywni XXI"

Duration 120 min Participants 12–20 people Audience Seniors 60+

About the Workshop

Participants explore the question of whether introducing the euro would bring more benefits than drawbacks, through a structured Oxford-style debate that develops civic dialogue skills.

The Debate Motion

"Introducing a common currency across the entire European Union would bring more benefits than drawbacks."

Central question: Should Poland adopt the euro instead of the zloty?

Workshop Objectives

General Objectives

  • 1Raise awareness of the introduction of the single currency, the euro, in all Member States.
  • 2Encourage active civic engagement and critical reflection on monetary union within the EU.

Specific Objectives

  • 1Understand the concept of adopting the euro as a national currency.
  • 2Develop argumentation, communication and critical thinking skills.
  • 3Learn the structure and principles of an Oxford-style debate.

Who Is This Workshop For?

Seniors aged 60+ interested in current social, economic and European issues. All bring valuable life experience and perspectives.

Working Methods

  • 1Oxford-style debate
  • 2Moderated discussion
  • 3Group work
  • 4Brainstorming

How an Oxford Debate Works

The Oxford-style debate is a structured and respectful way of discussing controversial issues. Two sides alternate in presenting arguments; the audience listens, asks questions and votes at the end.

The two sides

Proposition defends the motion; Opposition challenges it. A third group acts as the Audience / Jury.

Structure

Speakers alternate: 1st Proposition → 1st Opposition → 2nd Proposition → 2nd Opposition. The debate always closes with the 4th speaker from the Opposition.

Time limits

Each speaker has up to 3 minutes. A raised hand at 30 seconds remaining signals a warning; a second raise signals time is up.

The vote

At the end the audience votes on which side presented the more convincing arguments.

How the Workshop Unfolds (120 min)

  1. 1

    Introduction — Oxford Debate rules

    Welcome, presentation of the debate motion and explanation of the Oxford debate format: roles, rules.

    35–45 min

  2. 2

    Activity 1 — Preparing arguments

    Participants are divided into Proposition, Opposition and Audience/Jury. Each side prepares at least three key arguments. The Audience prepares 2–3 questions.

    20–25 min

  3. 3

    Activity 2 — The Oxford Debate

    The structured debate takes place: speakers alternate within the time limit. The Audience asks questions and votes at the end.

    30–40 min

  4. 4

    Evaluation and Closing

    Short verbal feedback round or written evaluation form. Key questions: What did you learn? What was most interesting? Did you feel comfortable?

    25–35 min
